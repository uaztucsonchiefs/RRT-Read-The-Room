Part 1 · the choreography

First learn the dance.
Then learn the medicine.

A rapid response is a team scrambled to a deteriorating ward patient to head off a code. Before any physiology — here's how one actually runs at Banner, because the choreography is half the battle.

OVERHEAD + CODE PAGER:  "RAPID RESPONSE · ADULT · [OLD / NEW TOWER] · FLOOR [X] · ROOM [X]"
How you hear it
Overhead, then the code pager. Be somewhere you can actually hear the overhead call.
Where they happen
Anywhere — rooms, imaging, endoscopy, dialysis, CARS; Old and New Tower.
Who's up
A rotating IM wards team (teams 1–6) carries rapid/code by day; night float covers overnight.

Banner – University Medical Center Tucson · IM rapid/code workflow.

The approach

Walk briskly. Do not run.

Stop what you're doing and head over right away — but don't sprint in. The heart rate you walk in with becomes the room's heart rate.

On the way
If it's a long walk, assign roles and rough out the likely case and first moves before you arrive.
At the door
Expect a crowd and the code cart (arrest meds + equipment). Take one breath; look at your team.
"Walking in: 'Rapid team's here — how can we help?' Your calm is contagious. So is your panic. Pick one."

Banner IM rapid response orientation.

If you're running it

Head of the bed. Eyes on patient and monitor. Talk out loud.

Set up
Stand at the head of the bed, assign roles, and lay eyes on the patient and the vitals at the same time.
Get the story
Ask the bedside RN why the rapid was called — your best source unless a physician's already there. Recheck vitals throughout.
Narrate
No one reads your mind. Say what you want and why; call out what's ruled in and ruled out. It builds the team's confidence and your own.
When stuck
Asking the room for ideas is a strength. You make the final call — credit good suggestions out loud, and it builds the team.
"Thinking out loud isn't insecurity. It's leadership with the volume turned up."

Banner IM rapid response leader guidance.

If you're support

Take your position. Close the loop. Write it down.

Pick your post
Bedside helping the leader think, or on the computer pulling the chart and placing orders — and writing down what was ordered.
Closed loop
"Please order a VBG with a lactic acid." → "VBG with lactic acid, ordered." Every order, every time.
Be the runner
Sometimes you're calling family or the MICU while MAs and RNs grab supplies.
"Stress deletes memory first. If it left someone's mouth as an order, it should land on paper."

Banner IM rapid response support roles.

Pop quiz · close the loop

You're the support resident on the computer. The leader calls out, "Order a VBG with a lactic acid." Safest response?

C · Close the loop

Repeat the order back so the whole room hears it landed, place it, then confirm out loud when it's done ("VBG's in"). It's the cheapest fix for the two classic rapid-response failures: the order nobody actually placed, and the order placed twice. And if you're on the keyboard, jot it down — stress deletes memory first.

Banner IM rapid response; closed-loop communication.

The exit

Don't leave a worried nurse behind.

Before you call it
Confirm with the whole team (RNs, SWAT, RT) that everything's been done to prevent an emergency, and that there's a clear plan the primary team knows. Never end with the bedside RN still worried.
You don't have to fix it all
Notify the primary team at the start; they take over. Then write a brief rapid note with the key details.
Debrief
If there's time, huddle: what went well, what to improve, and re-examine the differential — ideally with the whole team. Two minutes here makes the next rapid better.
"A rapid ends when there's a plan — not when you walk out the door."

Banner IM rapid response wrap-up & debrief.

Part 2 · the thinking · why rapids work

Patients announce the crash before they crash.

Up to ~80% of patients show abnormal vital signs in the 24 hours before a cardiac arrest, unplanned ICU transfer, or death. Yet 20–30% of ward patients receive substandard care before reaching the unit, and the majority of those events were potentially preventable.

The gap is rarely knowledge. It's noticing in time. That gap has a name: failure to rescue.

"The vitals were already in the chart. The problem was that nobody acted on them."

SCCM Recognizing & Responding to Deterioration guidelines (2023); classic deterioration cohorts.

Pop quiz · does this actually work?

An RRT rolls to the bedside. Is this saving lives — or just generating documentation?

B · The cavalry is real

Pooled RCT and meta-analytic data show rapid response teams cut hospital mortality by ~26% (RR ≈ 0.74) and non-ICU cardiac arrests by ~35% (RR ≈ 0.65) — in adults and children. A nationwide Dutch rollout across 166,000+ patients reduced the composite of arrest, unplanned ICU admission, or death. The catch: the system only works if you call it.

SCCM 2023; Chan et al., RRT meta-analysis, Arch Intern Med 2010; Dutch nationwide RRS trial.

What actually changes at the bedside

The earliest trigger isn't a number.
It's a nurse who's worried.

The most common real-world RRT triggers are low SpO₂, low systolic BP, altered mentation — and plain staff concern, which frequently fires before any value crosses a threshold. Intensivist-led teams also reduce ICU readmissions and prompt earlier, more appropriate goals-of-care conversations. An RRT isn't just resuscitation — it's a second brain in the room.

NEWS 0–4
Routine monitoring — keep watching.
Any single = 3
Red flag. One extreme value (e.g. AVPU not "Alert") earns urgent bedside review even if the total score is low.
NEWS 5–6
Urgent review by a clinician with acute-care competencies.
NEWS ≥ 7
Emergency response — activate the team, continuous monitoring, consider ICU.

NEWS2 (Royal College of Physicians); SCCM 2023.

Your instrument panel

Six gauges. They are not created equal.

Every rapid response begins the same way: you stare at the monitor. But the monitor weights every vital the same — and physiology doesn't. Some signs move early and predict hard. Others stay reassuringly normal while the patient circles the drain. The ranking, color-coded the way the monitor already trains your eye:

RRearliest to move · best single predictor
SpO₂powerful — when you can trust it
SBPheavy hitter when low · lies when "normal"
HRcontext-dependent · bradycardia ≠ safe
Mentationany change scores maximally
Tempweakest predictor of acute arrest

Matched case-control & nested cohort data on in-hospital arrest.

The protagonist

Respiratory rate is the smoke alarm.

It's usually the first vital to drift when a patient deteriorates, and as a single sign it out-predicts the rest (max RR AUC ≈ 0.72 vs. max HR ≈ 0.68; temperature is nearly useless for acute arrest). It's also the cheapest thing in the room — no probe, no cuff, no cost.

So naturally, it's the one we measure worst.

"Tachypnea is RR ≥ 20. Don't trust the chart — walk in, and count it yourself for a full minute."

MGH Housestaff Manual 2025–26, Respiratory Distress; Churpek et al.

Pop quiz · the fun one

Pull 37,000 charted respiratory rates from real inpatients. What does the distribution look like?

A · "Is everyone really breathing 20 times a minute?"

Across 36,966 hospitalizations, charted RR wasn't a bell curve — it was sharply right-skewed and piled onto 18 and 20 ("digit preference"), even in patients who were hypoxic or had cardiopulmonary disease. The number in the chart is frequently a polite fiction. The twist that flips the lesson: when humans actually count, that manual RR out-predicts the automated monitor for ICU transfer. So the takeaway isn't "trust the machine" — it's be the person who counts.

Badawy et al., BMJ Qual Saf 2017; Churpek et al., 2018.

The pecking order

One abnormal vital is bad.
The count is what kills.

In case-control data, the heaviest single hitters for impending arrest were severe hypotension (SBP ≤ 80, OR ≈ 15), a collapsing oxygenation ratio (OR ≈ 14), and NEWS2 ≥ 7 (OR ≈ 12). But the cleaner signal is the number of simultaneously deranged vitals — risk climbs stepwise with each one.

"Abnormal vitals are cockroaches — if you see one, there are more, and each one multiplies the risk."
Gauge #1 that lies · SpO₂

The pulse ox is confidently wrong
more often than you think.

Occult hypoxemia
Pulse oximetry overestimates SpO₂ in patients with darker skin — true hypoxemia is missed roughly 3× as often. A "94%" can hide a real desaturation.
Bad perfusion
In vasoconstriction or low flow, the number is garbage. If the pleth waveform is junk, so is the saturation. Get an ABG.
The calibration cliff
SpO₂ ~90% already means PaO₂ ~60 mmHg. Below 90%, small SpO₂ drops = large PaO₂ drops.
"Look at the pleth waveform before you believe the number on top of it."

Sjoding et al., NEJM 2020; JAMA 2022; MGH Manual, Hypoxemia & Hypercapnia.

Pop quiz · trust the number?

Four patients each read SpO₂ 92%. In which one is the true oxygenation most likely worse than the number suggests?

C · Two strikes against the number

Pulse oximetry tends to overestimate SpO₂ in patients with darker skin — occult hypoxemia is missed roughly three times as often — and a poor-perfusion, low-amplitude pleth makes any reading unreliable. Both apply here, so the real saturation is probably below 92%. When the waveform is junk or the stakes are high, confirm with an ABG. And mind the cliff: ~90% already means PaO₂ ~60.

Sjoding et al., NEJM 2020; JAMA 2022; MGH Manual, Hypoxemia.

Gauges #2 & #3 · BP + HR

"Stable vitals" is the most dangerous
phrase in the hospital.

Blood pressure
A normal BP does not rule out shock — young patients compensate right up until they fall off a cliff. Use the shock index (HR ÷ SBP): > 0.8 signals trouble even when each number looks fine alone. Track the trend, not the snapshot.
Heart rate
Cardiac output = HR × stroke volume, so bradycardia can be shock. Don't be soothed by a HR of 55 in a gray, mottled patient. And beware the inappropriately normal HR — the β-blocked or elderly patient who simply can't mount a tachycardia.
"Compensation is a patient lying politely. Believe the trend, not the moment."

IBCC (EMCrit) — Shock; MGH Manual, Shock ("any of these can be normal — including BP").

Pop quiz · the bedside
03:14 · RRT called. 72M, POD2. Monitor reads: BP 108/56 · HR 96 · SpO₂ 94% RA · Temp 37.1 · RR charted 18. He's more confused than this morning. The nurse "just feels off about him."

What is the single most important next move?

B · The "normal" numbers were camouflage

You count it: the RR is actually 28. Now re-read the room — new confusion + SBP ≈ 108 + RR > 22 lights up qSOFA (RR > 22, altered mentation, SBP ≤ 100). The charted RR was fiction; the soft BP was compensation; the nurse's gut was data. Two of three qSOFA criteria → treat as sepsis until proven otherwise and escalate now.

MGH Manual 2025–26, Sepsis (qSOFA); IBCC — Shock.

Off the monitor

The best perfusion monitors are
your eyes and your hand.

Mottling
Less sensitive, very specific, tracks mortality — and it usually starts on the knees. It signals endogenous vasoconstriction → think pump support, not just more squeeze.
Cap refill
Press a fingertip ~10s; <3s normal, >5s bad. Crude, free, and a validated resuscitation target (ANDROMEDA-SHOCK).
Cool limbs
All four cool beats any single one — an early sign of a clamped-down, low-output state.
Pulse pressure
Narrow (<25% of SBP) = low stroke volume; wide = vasodilation. The cuff already told you — if you read it.
"Touch the knees. The monitor can't."

IBCC (EMCrit) — Shock: bedside hemodynamic assessment.

The counterintuitive one

Lactate is an adrenaline meter,
not an oxygen meter.

We treat lactate like a tissue-hypoxia gauge. Usually it isn't — it mostly reflects endogenous epinephrine driving aerobic glycolysis in response to stress. Two consequences that flip the bedside logic:

A high lactate
Still an alarm. Read it as shock or impending disaster until proven otherwise — and a sudden jump in the anion gap in a hospitalized patient means the same thing.
A normal lactate
Not reassurance. A patient with a blunted sympathetic response can be in genuine shock with a perfectly normal lactate.
"Normal lactate is not a green light. It's just one quiet voice in a noisy room."

IBCC / PulmCrit (Farkas) — understanding lactate.

Pop quiz · the reassuring lab that isn't

A sick-looking patient with borderline-low BP. The lactate comes back normal at 1.4. How reassured are you?

D · Normal lactate ≠ green light

Lactate mostly tracks an endogenous epinephrine surge, not oxygen debt — so a patient who can't mount that response, or who is early, can be in genuine shock with a perfectly normal lactate. A high lactate is an alarm; a normal one is just one quiet voice in a noisy room. Trust the whole picture — perfusion, mentation, the trend — not a single comforting number.

IBCC / PulmCrit (Farkas).

The whole talk on one card

When the pager goes off — go R.A.P.I.D.

R · Rate

Shock index, the trend, the camouflage. Is "normal" actually compensated?
I · Integrate
Count how many vitals are off, and trust the worry in the room.
D · Decide & escalate
Calling for help — or intubating — is not a sign of weakness. Norepinephrine is the default pressor; just say no to dopamine.

Beyond the vitals · your toolkit

You've read the room.
Now reach for the right tool — fast ones first.

Vitals start the story; these finish it. Rank your tools by speed, because a deteriorating patient won't wait on the lab — and the quickest ones are free or nearly instant.

INSTANT
Your eyes & hands — re-examine after every intervention (exam, exam, exam). Fingerstick glucose — the one number you can't miss in anyone altered; hypoglycemia is a 30-second fix wearing a stroke costume.
MINUTES
VBG (pH, lytes, Hgb, lactate) · ECG → telemetry · portable CXR · POCUS if you've got the hands. Each one earns its keep.
SLOWER
Formal labs — CBC, CMP, troponin, cultures. Worth sending, but they take real time, so send them early.
"If you're already sticking them, send it all. The extra tube is free — the repeat stick is not."

MGH Housestaff Manual 2025–26; IBCC (EMCrit).

Pop quiz · the highest-yield stick
Hypotensive, tachypneic patient. The pleth tracing is crisp and SpO₂ reads 95%. You want pH, electrolytes, Hgb, and a lactate — and you want them now.

What do you send?

A · The VBG is the MVP

A peripheral VBG hands you pH, pCO₂, bicarb, electrolytes, hemoglobin, glucose, ionized calcium, and a lactate — usually in minutes. With a trustworthy pulse-ox waveform, you don't need the PaO₂ that only an ABG adds, so the ABG just buys pain and complications (and ~15% of "ABGs" are accidentally venous anyway). Pitfalls: a VBG won't give you accurate oxygenation, and the venous O₂ sat is your quality-control check — if it's bizarre, the gas may be unreliable. Save the arterial stick for when you truly need the PaO₂ or the pleth is junk.

IBCC / PulmCrit (Farkas), ABG vs VBG; MGH Manual, Respiratory Distress.

The pictures · CXR + ECG

One film, one tracing —
then put them on a leash.

Portable CXR · STAT
Pearls: a 60-second read of the big four — edema, infiltrate/aspiration, lobar collapse (mucus plug), pneumothorax. Pitfalls: a supine AP film hides effusions and small PTX, fakes cardiomegaly, and a normal film never rules out PE, early pneumonia, or ischemia.
12-lead ECG → telemetry
Pearls: catches ischemia, hyperkalemia, QT and blocks, and dangerous rhythms in seconds — the cheapest test that changes management fastest. Then leave them on the monitor: one ECG is a photograph; telemetry is the surveillance tape.
"A 12-lead is a snapshot. The next event is the one that kills — so catch it on tele."

MGH Manual 2025–26, Respiratory Distress & Telemetry.

The probe · POCUS

Ultrasound answers the question
the exam can't — if you ask it precisely.

In a crashing patient, a focused study (the RUSH idea — pump, tank, pipes) narrows undifferentiated shock in minutes, at the bedside, in real time.

What it nails · pearls
Pericardial effusion & tamponade, gross LV function, a big dilated RV (PE / RV strain), B-lines for pulmonary edema, lung sliding to rule out PTX, free fluid (FAST), and a plethoric vs. collapsing IVC for volume context.
Where it bites · pitfalls
It's operator-dependent — only as good as the hands on the probe. The IVC alone is an unreliable volume gauge; never decide on it in isolation. And the cardinal sin: scanning must not delay resuscitation.
"POCUS is a question you ask, not a picture you admire. One question, one answer, back to the patient."

IBCC (EMCrit); RUSH protocol.

Pop quiz · the probe's biggest trap

Your undifferentiated-shock patient is hypotensive and getting sicker. What's the single most dangerous way to use POCUS here?

D · The cardinal sin

POCUS earns its keep by answering one focused question fast — pump, tank, pipes. The danger is treating it as an imaging study to admire while the patient deteriorates in front of you. (Honorable mention: deciding volume status on the IVC alone, which is notoriously unreliable.) Ask, answer, get back to resuscitating.

IBCC (EMCrit); RUSH protocol.

The whole point of the consult

You're really hunting for
just three things.

Strip away the noise. Every rapid response is a search for one of three time-critical diagnoses. Find any of them, and the disposition writes itself.

Catch #1
Shock
Tissue isn't being perfused — and it loves hiding behind "normal" vitals. (See the entire first half of this talk.)
Catch #2
Respiratory failure
Active or impending. Can't oxygenate, can't ventilate, or simply tiring out in front of you.
Catch #3
Surgical abdomen
The catastrophe in the belly. So press on the abdomen — every single time.
"Shock. Respiratory failure. Surgical abdomen. Miss one of these and nothing else you did mattered."
Pop quiz · don't forget the belly

Hypotensive, tachycardic, lactate climbing, no clear source on your chest and cardiac workup. Which bedside move best catches what you're missing?

A · Press on the belly — every time

One of your three must-not-miss diagnoses is an acute surgical emergency, and the abdomen is the easiest source to overlook on a "medical" rapid response. A rigid, peritonitic, or pulsatile/expansile abdomen turns this into a surgical or vascular emergency and an immediate consult — no volume of fluid or pressor fixes a perforated viscus or a ruptured AAA.

The goal: shock · respiratory failure · surgical abdomen.

Disposition · who needs the unit

When the floor can't hold them.

The unit is for patients whose physiology is outrunning the resources on the ward. Persistent hypotension is one trigger (next slide) — here are the rest:

The eyeball test
Anyone who simply looks like they're dying. Your gestalt is a validated vital sign — if your gut says ICU, listen to it.
Can't wean the NRB
Maxed-out oxygen with nowhere left to go on the floor. Needing HFNC / NIV / intubation is needing the unit.
Unstable bradycardia
Atropine 1 mg (q3–5 min, max 3 mg). No response? → epinephrine 2–10 mcg/min (or dopamine 5–20 mcg/kg/min) and get the pacing pads on.
Unstable tachyarrhythmia
Synchronized cardioversion — now. In a truly unstable patient, don't dawdle chasing the rhythm with drugs.
"If you're asking whether they need the ICU, you've usually already answered the question."

MGH Manual 2025–26, ACLS Bradycardia & Tachycardia.

Pop quiz · unstable and fast

Narrow-complex tachycardia at 190. The patient is now hypotensive, diaphoretic, and confused. Your move?

C · Instability rewrites the algorithm

The moment a tachyarrhythmia produces shock, altered mentation, ischemic chest pain, or acute heart failure, it's unstable — and the answer is immediate synchronized cardioversion, not a drug trial. Adenosine and rate control are for the stable patient with time to spare. Sedate if you can, but don't make a hypotensive, obtunded patient wait on pharmacology.

MGH Manual 2025–26, ACLS Tachycardia.

Persistent hypotension · the floor play

Before you flood them —
ask if fluid will even help.

1 · TEST IT
Passive leg raise. Lay them flat and lift the legs ~45° — a reversible ~300 mL "autotransfusion." A real bump in BP/perfusion = fluid-responsive; nothing = fluid won't fix this. Free, instant, and it undoes itself.
2 · DOSE SMALL
If you give fluid, use LR (balanced) over NS — and give less than you think. Provocatively, much of a bolus's BP bump may be the cold fluid triggering an endogenous catecholamine surge, not durable volume — and it extravasates fast. Albumin is reasonable in select patients (e.g. cirrhosis). Fluid is a drug; dose it.
3 · PRESSOR
Start norepinephrine peripherally through a good IV — don't wait for a central line. It's your most predictable move and your ticket to the unit. When in doubt, norepi.
"A bolus that 'worked' for ten minutes didn't work. Test, dose small, then norepi."

PulmCrit "Myth-busting the fluid bolus" (Farkas); IBCC — peripheral vasopressors.

Pop quiz · the bolus that "worked"

You give a hypotensive floor patient a 1 L bolus. The BP rises nicely — then drifts back down 15 minutes later. What does that tell you, and what's next?

D · Test, dose small, then norepi

A bolus effect that evaporates in minutes is the tell: much of the bump is a cold-fluid-triggered catecholamine surge plus rapid extravasation, not durable preload. A passive leg raise would have predicted this for free. Stop chasing liters — which mostly buy edema — and move to a peripheral norepinephrine drip: your most predictable move and your ticket to the unit.

PulmCrit "Myth-busting the fluid bolus" (Farkas); IBCC.

The executive summary

The entire talk, on one screen.

① Assess
Count the RR yourself — the chart is fiction, and it's the earliest, best single vital. "Stable vitals" lie: use shock index & trends; BP, HR, and SpO₂ can all read normal in a dying patient. Touch the knees (mottling, cap refill) — and a normal lactate is not reassurance.
② Tools · fast first
Exam → fingerstick → VBG (pH, lytes, Hgb, lactate; ABG rarely needed) → ECG + tele, CXR → POCUS (one focused question). If you're sticking them, send it all.
③ Find these three
Shock · Respiratory failure · Surgical abdomen (press on the belly). ICU if: looks like death, can't wean the NRB, persistent hypotension, or an unstable rhythm (brady → atropine then epi/pace; unstable tachy → sync cardiovert).
④ Always
Hypotension play: PLR → small balanced fluid → peripheral norepi. Trust the worried nurse. Escalate early — asking for help is not weakness.
